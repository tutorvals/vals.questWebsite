# Word definitions/examples

This page to easily reference particular conceptual understand and definitions of the words I use.

- caveats
    
    I mean not to disallow others using different meanings and care to understand what others meant by their own usage, and in fact I will occasionally use variant colloquial meanings even for words listed here as appropriate. 
    

Value vs Want vs Expect [unfinished draft]

Unmet expectations or wants cause (or are) suffering. While you want/expect the pain to go away, or the rain to stop, you suffer. When you accept and let it through, you suffer less (and not at all, at higher levels of acceptance). This is a valid reason to want or expect less things, to the extent you value suffering less. On the other hand, if wanting things is what motivates you to take action towards getting them, then extinguishing the want will make you get less of the thing you value. 

A skillful relation to what you value, want and expect will lead you to a more productive happy fulfilled life, so here I sketch out their uses and setting. At my current place in life, this is the best conceptual understanding and practical use I have for these words.

First, ~definitions.

Expecting something means you think the thing *should* happen, it’s weird and unsatisfying if it does not happen. I expected 

- Alternative meanings of Expecting
    
    In colloquial use expecting can be the thing that you do that when unmet leads to surprise. I expected an empty hallway, but here was a friend jumping out at me. I expected a normal continuation, but it ended up as a joke and I laughed. 
    

- “Sorry” in english | ”désolé” in french
    1. Used to convey there’s something I don’t like about this situation, it could have been better. Eg. you missed your flight, I’m sorry about that, I’m thinking it’d have been nicer if you didn’t.
    Eg. you tripped and your donut fell on the ground, that’s a pity.
    2. Used to convey moral fault/blame in a situation, towards myself. 
    Eg. I’m late to an important thing, shouldn’t have, and am causing some harm because of that. 
    
    It’s more often the case that sorry is used in the first definition, as in the second case I generally focus on solving the harm/reparing/doing something so it doesn’t happen again.
